CEAEval: Context-Aware Evaluation of Expressiveness Appropriateness in Speech


Codes are temporarily open-sourced anonymously.

Overview

Evaluating expressive speech becomes increasingly important as speech technologies advance; however, existing systems mainly assess emotional intensity and overlook whether a speech sample is expressively appropriate for its contextual setting. To address this limitation, we propose CEAEval, a context-aware framework for assessing speech expressive appropriateness. We first define the task of context-aware expressive appropriateness evaluation, which determines whether a speech sample expressively aligns with the content, situational context, and narrative intent under a given rich context. We then construct the first context-rich speech dataset for this task, CEAEval-D, which provides narrative descriptions together with fourteen categories of human annotations covering expressive attributes and overall appropriateness. Finally, we develop CEAEval-M, a model that integrates knowledge distillation, multi-model collaboration, audio attention biasing, and reinforcement learning to perform context-aware expressive appropriateness evaluation. Experiments on a human-annotated test set show that CEAEval-M outperforms existing speech evaluation and analysis systems. All data, models, and code are released at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Eval-DA0C.

Task Define

Figure 1. A narrative example illustrating how expressive intent emerges from conversational context.



As illustrated in Figure 1, the task of evaluating speech expressive appropriateness under rich contextual settings aims to assess whether a speech sample appropriately conveys expressiveness given a rich narrative context and a corresponding spoken utterance. The expressiveness appropriateness is rated on a scale from 0 to 5. In addition, the model produces a coherent reasoning process, including the analysis of paralinguistic cues, such that the resulting scores and rationales can serve as references for other expressive speech tasks.

CEAEval-D: Dataset

Figure 2. Statistical distribution of annotation categories and attributes in the CEAEval-D dataset.



We recruit 18 graduate students with backgrounds in speech emotion research to annotate the selected 16.1 hours of data. The annotations cover multiple aspects, including speech segment boundaries, speaker identity, textual content, expressive appropriateness scores, intonation, rhythm, emotion categories, recording conditions, the presence of background music, synthesis difficulty when treated as a text-to-speech task, speaker gender and age, as well as paralinguistic vocalizations and sound events. Detailed statistics of the annotated attributes are illustrated in Figure 2.

CEAEval-D: Dataset

Figure 3. Overview of the proposed three-stage training pipeline for context-aware speech expressiveness evaluation.



The judge model takes textual context and a speech segment as input and assesses the appropriateness of speech expressiveness under the given context. The overall training pipeline is illustrated in Figure 3. First, we distill audio description capabilities from Qwen3-Captioner using 3,505 hours of unlabeled speech data. We then introduce the Qwen3-8B LLM to predict ideal speech expressiveness based solely on textual context. Building on this, we train a Speech-LLM to score expressive appropriateness by performing chain-of-thought (CoT) style comparisons between the predicted ideal expressiveness and the evaluated speech, under an attention biasing mechanism. Finally, we apply reinforcement learning to further improve scoring accuracy. Through this three-stage training procedure, the model learns to evaluate speech expressive appropriateness under rich contextual information.
